Order Help: From 0.76 Extra Contacts Per Order to a Self-Service Platform

What started as a crisis became a platform. After Vistaprint's first Order Help launch created 0.76 extra contacts per order instead of reducing them, I led the end-to-end redesign and expansion of the tool — turning a failed product into a self-service system that deflected an increasing share of post-order contacts and enabled fully automated issue resolution across order tracking, damaged products, and late or missing deliveries.

Across all phases of this project, I analysed 200+ support cases, conducted usability testing with 35+ users, designed and tested four distinct flows, led discovery independently when PM support was unavailable, and pushed back on decisions that would have undermined the product's operational integrity — one of which was reversed by leadership as a result.

Challenge

In December 2024, Vistaprint launched Order Help (V1) to enable customers to manage orders without contacting support. Within weeks, the tool was creating 0.76 extra contacts per order instead of reducing them. It was rolled back after one month, and I was brought in to diagnose what went wrong and design a solution.

User problem

Users were promised "Get support quickly on your own," but 82% ended up waiting 24 hours for email responses. The flow didn't reveal this until the final step, leading users to abandon and use faster channels (chat/phone), creating follow-up cases. Out of 50 follow-up cases analysed, 23 were customers who didn't wait for email and moved to chat immediately.

Business problem

While the tool achieved 0.91% contact deflection through automated flows, this was completely negated by the 0.76 extra contacts it generated. The business needed to deflect 0.46% of post-order volume to reduce support costs, but the current solution was creating more work, not less. Operational issues compounded the problem: out of 200 cases reviewed, 98 had specialists asking for photos that customers had already uploaded.

Scope

The business needed a self-service tool that would reduce customer effort and deflect support contacts without creating operational burden. The solution had to set clear expectations (no false promises of instant resolution), offer channel flexibility (let users choose their preferred contact method), and be operationally feasible given limited automation capabilities. Based on stakeholder workshops, I established four design principles: simplicity first, clear expectations, channel flexibility, and operational feasibility.

A decision I pushed back on

Early in the project, there was pressure to quickly patch V1 rather than rethink the flow. I pushed back, arguing that the data — the follow-up case patterns, the back-and-forth email volume, the photo re-request rate — pointed to structural problems, not surface ones. Fixing copy and loading states wouldn't address the fact that the tool was generating contacts, not deflecting them. The team aligned on a full redesign.

Later, a decision was made not to create Salesforce cases when automated resolutions were issued — meaning specialists would have no visibility into what the system had done on a customer's behalf. I escalated this with other UX designers in the domain, and we presented the operational risk to stakeholders collectively. The decision was reversed.

Phase 2 — Q1–Q2: Expanding the Platform

From relaunch to platform

After the relaunch, the product triad reviewed support case data and identified two patterns that pointed to further automation opportunities. First, a large volume of contacts were simple order status enquiries — customers asking where their order was, a question the system could answer directly. Second, the most frequent specialist resolutions for product quality issues followed a consistent pattern: offer a reprint, refund, or store credit. If the logic was that consistent, it could be automated.

We prioritised the expansion roadmap together as a triad. Order tracking launched in Q1. Damaged products and late/missing products launched in Q2. For the final two flows, I led discovery independently — the PM was not available to support those projects, so I ran stakeholder interviews, analytics review, prioritisation, and research coordination myself. Stakeholders accepted and moved both forward.

How the three expansion flows were sequenced across Q1 and Q2

Order tracking (Q1)

"Where is my order" was one of the highest-volume contact reasons. The tracking flow surfaces live order status directly within Order Help — customers can see whether each product is in transit, out for delivery, or has had a delivery attempt — and from there initiate a delivery issue report if needed. 10 users were tested on the flow, all 10 completed the task successfully (100% task completion rate), and 9 out of 10 reported being very confident they'd found the right information.

The most significant outcome: 67% of customers who view their order status choose not to create a support ticket at all. Seeing the information resolves their concern. This figure is now tracked as part of the platform's ongoing deflection metrics.

A design constraint that required an A/B test

The order status screen presented a genuine tension between design system constraints and contextual needs. The design system team encouraged us to reuse an existing component; the PM domain lead felt the solution didn't suit the experience of viewing tracking information in a support context. Rather than letting the disagreement become a stalemate, I ran an A/B test to establish whether users had a preference. Both solutions performed comparably. This gave the engineering team data-backed grounds to make the final call, and it removed subjectivity from what had become a stakeholder conflict.

Finding: Minor friction when users with multiple orders couldn't immediately identify which order had a delivery issue

Response: Flagged for future improvement as part of Help Center redesign — Self Service Card anatomy work

Finding: A magnifying glass icon on product images caused confusion — users assumed it was interactive

Response: Recommended removing the icon to eliminate the false affordance

Damaged products (Q2)

When a customer's product arrives damaged, the flow guides them through describing the issue, uploading a photo, and selecting a resolution — reprint, refund, or store credit. This replaced a pattern where specialists were manually reviewing cases and offering the same three options, often after requesting photos the customer had already submitted through Order Help. Designing this flow required close collaboration with the Process and Operations team to ensure the automated resolutions aligned with Vistaprint's policies and the conditions under which each option was permissible.

10 users tested the flow. Overall satisfaction was extremely high — several gave the experience a perfect satisfaction score. The usability testing also uncovered a specific insight about issue labelling: the category "Product is broken" confused users testing with an apparel scenario, as the concept of "broken" didn't map intuitively onto fabric. This informed a copy refinement. The "Damaged package" label also caused ambiguity, with users uncertain whether it referred to the shipping box or the product packaging. Both were addressed before finalisation.

Finding: "Product is broken" felt inapplicable to soft goods like T-shirts

Response: Refined copy to better map to the full range of product types Vistaprint sells

Finding: "Damaged package" was interpreted inconsistently — some read it as the shipping box, others as the product packaging

Response: Clarified the label to remove ambiguity

Finding: Users valued being able to choose a resolution (replacement vs refund) and appreciated seeing an estimated reprint delivery date, which helped them decide

Response: Retained the delivery date display as a core part of the resolution selection screen

Late and missing products (Q2)

The late and missing flow handles two emotionally distinct but related scenarios. If a product is still in transit but past its promised delivery date, the customer is offered a shipping fee refund or store credit — acknowledging the delay without over-committing on a resolution for a product that hasn't been lost yet. If the system shows "Delivered" but the customer reports it never arrived, the full product cost is refunded. This resolution logic was built in close coordination with the Process team, which proposed additional rules after the flow launched and continued to evolve the automation criteria.

Discovery for this flow followed a full research process: analytics review via Looker, stakeholder interviews to understand operational policies, Jobs to Be Done mapping, benchmarking, brainstorming, concept evaluation, and A/B testing of two late-products variants — all completed in under three weeks. Usability testing with 5 users validated the core flow but surfaced a key gap: when users whose product genuinely hadn't arrived saw that their only self-service options were shipping compensation, most chose to contact customer support instead. The resolution options were insufficient for the emotional weight of the scenario.

Finding: "Products arrived later than promised" caused momentary confusion alongside "Products never arrived" — users weren't sure which to pick

Response: Simplified to "Products arrived late" to reduce cognitive load at a high-frustration moment

Finding: For genuinely missing high-value items, shipping-only compensation was seen as inadequate — users defaulted to contacting support

Response: Recommended integrating full product refund/reprint options for the "never arrived" scenario in the next iteration

Impact

Growing deflection across the platform

The platform grew from 0.3% deflection in May — the baseline before the relaunch — to 1.5% and still climbing as all automated flows reached steady state. The original business target was 0.46%. The 6.5% follow-up case rate from V1 was eliminated. The 0.76 extra contacts per order was reversed.

The order tracking flow alone accounts for a meaningful share of that deflection: 67% of users who check their order status do not go on to create a support ticket, meaning the tool is resolving a large volume of contacts simply by surfacing information that customers previously had to call or chat to obtain.

Reduced customer effort

Across all four flows, usability testing consistently showed users completing tasks with high confidence, low friction, and often a tone of genuine surprise at how easy the process was. Users contrasted Order Help favourably with Amazon and other platforms. Several noted they were "pleased not to need to call customer service." The damaged products flow received a perfect 10/10 satisfaction score from multiple participants.

Operational integrity preserved

The Salesforce case visibility issue — which would have left specialists unable to see what automated resolutions had been issued — was caught and reversed before launch. This protected both the customer experience (no conflicting communications from agents unaware of prior automated actions) and the operational team's ability to audit and improve the system over time.

The modular platform structure enabling continued expansion

A foundation for continued expansion

The modular design approach — prioritising non-automated flows first to reduce Average Handle Time, then automation for deflection — meant the platform could expand without rebuilding. Each new flow reused shared components, shared the same entry point, and plugged into the same Salesforce and Process team infrastructure. The deflection number continues to grow.

Result

In under a year, Order Help went from a rolled-back product that was making support worse to a multi-flow self-service platform delivering measurable, growing deflection. The work required diagnosing a failed launch, rebuilding trust with stakeholders, designing and testing four distinct flows, leading discovery independently when PM support was unavailable, and navigating operational and design system constraints without letting them compromise the quality of the customer experience.
