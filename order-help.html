Order Help V2: From Crisis to 2% Deflection

This project resulted in a self-service tool that deflected ~2% of post-order support contacts and reduced customer effort. I transformed a failed product that created 0.76 extra contacts per order into a solution that surpassed business goals and enabled automated order management.

Within this project, I analysed 200+ support cases to diagnose failure modes, conducted usability testing with 10 users, designed three solution proposals, and collaborated with stakeholders across CARE leadership, operations, and product to align on a feasible path forward.

Challenge

In December 2024, Vistaprint launched Order Help (V1) to enable customers to manage orders without contacting support. Within weeks, the tool was creating 0.76 extra contacts per order instead of reducing them. It was rolled back after one month, and I was brought in to diagnose what went wrong and design a solution.

User problem

Users were promised "Get support quickly on your own," but 82% ended up waiting 24 hours for email responses. The flow didn't reveal this until the final step, leading users to abandon and use faster channels (chat/phone), creating follow-up cases. Out of 50 follow-up cases analysed, 23 were customers who didn't wait for email and moved to chat immediately.

Business problem

While the tool achieved 0.91% contact deflection through automated flows, this was completely negated by the 0.76 extra contacts it generated. The business needed to deflect 0.46% of post-order volume to reduce support costs, but the current solution was creating more work, not less. Operational issues compounded the problem: out of 200 cases reviewed, 98 had specialists asking for photos that customers had already uploaded.

Process

Research

  • Support case analysis (200+ cases via Salesforce)
  • Follow-up case deep dive (50 cases)
  • Customer support specialist interviews
  • Workshop with CARE leadership
  • Usability testing (10 users, UK, ages 40–60)

Findings

  • Broken promise of speed: 82% of users expected instant help but got 24-hour email wait times
  • Late discovery of delays: Users only learned about the 24-hour wait at the final step, causing abandonment
  • Operational failures: 98 out of 200 cases had specialists requesting photos already uploaded; specialists closed cases before the customer replied
  • Email created back-and-forth: 0.56 extra emails per order due to missing information or unclear specialist responses

Scope

The business needed a self-service tool that would reduce customer effort and deflect support contacts without creating operational burden. The solution had to set clear expectations (no false promises of instant resolution), offer channel flexibility (let users choose their preferred contact method), and be operationally feasible given limited automation capabilities. Based on stakeholder workshops, I established four design principles: simplicity first, clear expectations, channel flexibility, and operational feasibility.

Ideate

  • Three solution proposals exploring different trade-offs
  • Mid-fidelity wireframes for stakeholder alignment
  • Evaluation matrix: Usability, Feasibility, Customer Expectations

Proposal A (Selected): Non-automated flows prepare detailed cases and hand them to CARE via the user's preferred channel. Strong usability and customer-centricity, moderate dev effort due to Chat Anywhere dependency.

Proposal B: Minimal flow to contact cards with order number prepopulated. Faster implementation but adds friction with extra screens before users can contact support.

Proposal C: Automation-only approach with no case creation for non-automated reasons. High feasibility and clear expectations, but limited coverage given current automation constraints.

Test

  • Usability testing with 10 UK users (ages 40–60) via UserTesting
  • Analysis in HeyMarvin to identify patterns
  • Iterative design updates based on findings

Key test findings & design responses

Finding: Users expected human support for cancellations and were confused by "24 hours" messaging

Response: Removed "24 hours" from success screens, added clear confirmation that the order was cancelled

Finding: "Get help with this order" button was not prominent enough

Response: Improved visual hierarchy, ensured "View all products" didn't compete for attention

Finding: "Submit as email" made users think they needed to exit the flow

Response: Changed copy to "Continue to submit via email" for clarity

Stakeholder alignment

Throughout the project, I navigated complex stakeholder dynamics to ensure buy-in across CARE leadership, operations, product, and engineering. The hardest challenge was aligning diverse expectations while maintaining design quality.

To address concerns, I:

  • Presented three proposals with clear trade-offs (usability vs. feasibility vs. customer expectations) to facilitate informed decision-making
  • Led stakeholder presentations explaining the value proposition and business impact of each approach
  • Facilitated workshops with CARE leadership to understand operational constraints and validate automation feasibility
  • Collaborated closely with engineering to ensure design system components were technically feasible and maintainable
  • Co-authored PRDs with PM to align on success metrics and rollout strategy

Impact

Reduced customer effort & improved clarity

By removing false promises of instant resolution, improving visual hierarchy, and clarifying copy throughout the flow, users reported high confidence in the updated experience. Usability testing showed most users understood cancellation processes, contact expectations, and remediation options clearly.

Surpassed business goals

Order Help V2 achieved ~2% sustained contact deflection, significantly surpassing the 0.46% goal. The solution reduced back-and-forth emails, eliminated the 6.5% follow-up case rate, and enabled expansion with additional automated flows (order tracking, late/missing/damaged product resolution).

[Metrics dashboard showing deflection performance]

Performance metrics showing sustained 2% contact deflection

Foundation for scalable automation

The modular design approach enabled quick expansion post-launch. By prioritising non-automated flows first (to reduce Average Handle Time) and automation second (for deflection), we balanced quick wins with long-term strategic value. This created a foundation for adding new automated capabilities without rebuilding the entire system.

Result

This project resulted in a launched self-service tool that transformed a failed product into a business success. Order Help V2 achieved ~2% sustained contact deflection, reduced customer effort through clearer expectations, and created a foundation for scalable automation. The modular design enabled post-launch expansion with order tracking and automated issue resolution flows.
